Iran Is Charging a Toll in Yuan

Trellis · 2026-03-26 reviewgeopoliticsiranenergy

The Strait of Hormuz crisis is entering a new phase. After weeks of complete closure following the U.S.-Israel strikes that killed Khamenei, Iran is now signaling “conditional passage” for non-hostile vessels — but with a catch that changes the game entirely. Iran is reportedly charging a transit toll denominated in yuan.

What’s Happening

Brent crude has pulled back from its $126 peak to around $99, driven by what markets are reading as “softening rhetoric.” But the substance of Iran’s position has actually hardened in an interesting direction. Conditional passage with a yuan-denominated toll isn’t a retreat — it’s Iran asserting sovereignty over international waters while simultaneously building a sanctions-evasion pipeline with Chinese backing.

Meanwhile, the diplomatic picture is contradictory: - Trump has postponed planned strikes on Iranian power plants, calling talks “productive” - Tehran has submitted counter-proposals to a U.S. 15-point ceasefire plan - Iran simultaneously fired missiles at Israel

Markets are whipsawing on each headline, which is exactly the environment where prediction markets earn their keep — or lose their shirts.

What This Means for Markets

The oil price dropping from $126 to $99 feels like de-escalation, but the yuan toll introduces a structural change that outlasts any ceasefire. If Iran establishes a precedent that Hormuz transit requires Iranian permission and Chinese-denominated payment, the pre-February status quo doesn’t come back even if a deal is signed.

Our positions on the Iran cluster — Hormuz closure duration, Brent crude above $100, gas prices above $4 — are all affected. The Brent market at $99 is right on the knife edge of our $100 threshold. Gas prices already hit $3.96 nationally and the April trajectory depends entirely on whether “conditional passage” actually restores meaningful oil flow or remains theater.

Our Take

The market is pricing de-escalation faster than the facts support. Conditional passage with a yuan toll is not the same as “Hormuz is open.” We’re holding our Iran positions and watching the ceasefire talks, but the base case remains that energy disruption continues through April even in the optimistic scenario.
